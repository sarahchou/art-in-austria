Meander

By: Caroline Hegg
July 12, 2019

The meander pattern motif is defined as a continuous line that is repeated asa pattern, typically found as a decorative border on tile, vases, textiles, borders around illustrations, and buildings. It is also sometimes referred to as “Greek key” or “Greek fret.” Though widely connected to the ancient Greeks and sub-sequent historical classical revivals in art and architecture, the geometric simplicity and replicability of the motif almost ensured that it would be “invented” by cultures across the world.

Historical Use

The word “meander” derives from the Greek “Maíandros,” their name for theMeander River in present-day Turkey. The winding nature of the river gives meaning to the modern word “meander,” which means “to wander a windingcourse.” The meander pattern lends itself to these definitions, as it is identified by a continuous line that winds around itself in a linear pattern.

Ancient Greece and Rome: Though found in several cultures around the globe, the meander motif is most heavily associated with the ancient Greeks, who adorned such famous architectural works as the Parthenon, the Ara Pacis, and the Temple of Marsi. The motif has been found on countless archeological finds from vases to textiles. It was often found as a border to Greek, friezes, orlong, narrow bands of sculpture found on architectural worksii. This motif was also one of the popular motifs adopted by artists and architects paying tribute to the classics; during the Renaissance, in the art nouveau movement, and countless other examples from around the world.

China: The meander motif can also be found in Chinese decoration, in combination with other motifs such as lotus scroll, forming a “floral meander”iii (Figure 2).

Mesoamerica: Meander-like motifs have been identified in what is referred to as “Early-Middle Post classic Aztec” archaeologic finds (ceramics, architecture)iv. Meaning

Spiritual/Philosophical: The meander motif has held parallel representative meaning across the cultures that have used it. In Greece and Rome, it symbolized unity and infinity because of the continuous line aspect of the pattern. As mentioned above, the Aztec people are believed to have regarded it as a symbol of balance and reciprocal motion. Some accounts have even connected the meander motif to rotational motifs found in ancient Chinese and other Asian cultures. György Darvas made the connection between the rotational motifs represented by fish chasing their own tails (and later the yin yang symbol, which symbolized the unity between the opposing Tao and Confucian philosophies) and whirlpool motifs in Crete that went on to transform from rotational to translational, which resembled what we know as the meander motifvi. This connection elicits an unintentional unity between the two cultures, and gives more significance to the two patterns.

Geometry/Grammar: The visual simplicity of the meander motif lends question to the significance of it in the contexts of geometry, recursion, individual artistic influence, and design process. John S Gero and Mary Lou Mahler explore these questions in Computational and Cognitive Models of Creative Design VviiI. They conclude the geometric rules that govern meander motifs have significance that goes beyond surface aesthetic, and that these design are not random, but follow sets of rules that are also used in variations of the motif.

They also discovered that different forms, rules, and variations were specific to not only region and time period, but individual painters and artisans that used this motif in their workviii. This is testament to the notion that simple and ubiquitous motifs, as this one, can be just as artistically relevant as representational forms, and are of stylistic importance.
