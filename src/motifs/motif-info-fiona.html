Stupa

By: Fiona Gridley
July 12, 2019
Origin
The stupa is a type of Buddhist monument which holds religious relics and serves as a place of
worship. The stupa originated before Buddhism, and was typically a simple mound of earth
containing ashes or remnants from the bodies of kings or leaders/teachers. These mounds of
earth were often covered with stone to contain the relics. This tradition of a solid mound of earth
surrounded by a protective covering remains in Buddhist traditions, although the subject they
are meant to honor changes.
While the Buddha was still alive, he asked for stupas to be built as a place of worship and
meditation. He wanted the focus to be on reminding his followers of the Four Noble Truths and
the goal of enlightenment instead of specifically honoring the Buddha himself. After the Buddha
died, stupas were built at locations of important events during the Buddha’s life, as well as
across the Mauryan Dynasty (regions of modern-day India, Nepal, Bangladesh, Pakistan and
Afghanistan). The Buddha asked for stupas to be built not only at locations important to his life
so that more people would be reminded of his teachings. Originally, stupas were meant to hold
the earthly remains of the Buddha, but eventually all of the Buddha’s remains had been divided
between stupas and the stupa’s contents expanded to religious relics such as texts. Now,
stupas exist all across the world as places for Buddhist followers to worship and meditate.
Structure
Stupas always consist of a solid earth mound, which contains the religious relics or remains of
the Buddha, and some sort of outer covering. The basic structure of a Buddhist stupa includes
three main parts: the square base, which represents the Buddha’s legs as he is sitting in a
meditative pose, the round middle portion, which represents his body, and the smaller top
structure, which represents the Buddha’s head. Different regions and time periods constructed
different styles of stupas. These styles include that of The Great Stupa, built in Sanchi, India
beginning in the 3rd century BCE which is shorter and more round or that of Phra Si Ratana
Chedi built in Bangkok, Thailand in the 19th century, which is quite tall and completely covered
in gold.
Worship
Regardless of their shape or covering, stupas were built as places of worship and remembrance
of the Buddha’s teachings. Since stupas are completely solid, Buddhist practitioners
circumambulate, or walk around the structure instead of entering as one would a church or
temple. This circulating movement represents the cycle of rebirth and helps the worshipper think
of the process of enlightenment as a circular movement towards the stable center of the stupa.
This circular movement also increases the worshipper’s body temperature, which mimics the fire
involved in the process of cremating the Buddha, which relieved him of all worldly existence and
possessions.
